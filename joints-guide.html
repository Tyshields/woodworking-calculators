Complete reference for joinery techniques - strength, difficulty, and best uses

Butt Joint

BEGINNER
Strength:

The simplest joint where two pieces of wood meet end-to-end or end-to-face. Requires no special cuts, just square ends that meet at 90 degrees.

‚úì Pros

  • Easiest to make
  • No special tools needed
  • Fast assembly
  • Good for beginners

‚úó Cons

  • Weakest joint
  • Requires reinforcement
  • Limited glue surface
  • Not great for stress

Best For:

Face frames, simple boxes, framing, projects with pocket screws or dowels for reinforcement

Tools Needed:

Saw, square, screws/nails, glue

Miter Joint

BEGINNER
Strength:

Two pieces cut at matching angles (typically 45¬∞) that meet to form a corner. Hides end grain for a clean, professional appearance.

‚úì Pros

  • Clean, finished look
  • Hides end grain
  • Perfect for frames
  • Professional appearance

‚úó Cons

  • Moderate strength
  • Can gap if not precise
  • Needs accurate cuts
  • Requires clamping

Best For:

Picture frames, crown molding, door/window trim, decorative boxes

Tools Needed:

Miter saw or miter box, clamps, glue, (optional: splines or biscuits)

Dado Joint

INTERMEDIATE
Strength:

A flat-bottomed groove cut across the grain of one board to accept the edge of another. Creates a strong mechanical connection with lots of glue surface.

‚úì Pros

  • Very strong
  • Large glue surface
  • Supports weight well
  • Self-aligning

‚úó Cons

  • Requires router or dado blade
  • Must be precise width
  • Visible from side
  • Takes more time

Best For:

Bookshelves, cabinet shelves, drawer dividers, plywood cabinets

Tools Needed:

Router with straight bit, dado blade for table saw, or multiple passes with regular blade

Mortise & Tenon

ADVANCED
Strength:

The gold standard of woodworking joints. A rectangular projection (tenon) fits into a matching hole (mortise). Used for centuries in fine furniture and timber framing.

‚úì Pros

  • Extremely strong
  • Very durable
  • Large glue surface
  • Traditional/quality look

‚úó Cons

  • Time-consuming
  • Requires precision
  • Needs special tools
  • Steep learning curve

Best For:

Table legs and aprons, chair frames, door frames, quality furniture construction

Tools Needed:

Chisel, drill, router, mortiser (optional), tenon saw or table saw

Dovetail Joint

ADVANCED
Strength:

Interlocking trapezoidal "tails" and "pins" that resist being pulled apart. The hallmark of fine craftsmanship, often used as a visible design element.

‚úì Pros

  • Strongest corner joint
  • Mechanical locking
  • Beautiful aesthetic
  • No fasteners needed

‚úó Cons

  • Very difficult
  • Time-intensive
  • Requires practice
  • Special jigs helpful

Best For:

Fine drawer boxes, jewelry boxes, heirloom furniture, showcasing craftsmanship

Tools Needed:

Dovetail saw, chisels, marking gauge, dovetail jig (for router method)

Box/Finger Joint

INTERMEDIATE
Strength:

Interlocking rectangular "fingers" cut into both pieces. Easier than dovetails but still provides excellent strength and lots of glue surface area.

‚úì Pros

  • Very strong
  • Large glue area
  • Attractive appearance
  • Easier than dovetails

‚úó Cons

  • Needs table saw jig
  • Visible end grain
  • Takes practice
  • Must be precise

Best For:

Drawers, boxes, cases, decorative projects, shop-made storage

Tools Needed:

Table saw with dado blade, box joint jig (easy to make)

Dowel Joint

INTERMEDIATE
Strength:

Wooden pegs (dowels) fit into aligned holes in both pieces to strengthen and align the joint. A modern alternative to mortise and tenon.

‚úì Pros

  • Stronger than butt joint
  • No visible fasteners
  • Good alignment
  • Clean appearance

‚úó Cons

  • Holes must align perfectly
  • Needs dowel jig
  • Can be fussy
  • Hard to fix errors

Best For:

Edge-to-edge boards, frame corners, cabinet face frames, table tops

Tools Needed:

Drill, dowel jig, dowel centers, dowels, glue

Pocket Hole Joint

BEGINNER
Strength:

Angled holes drilled into one piece allow screws to be driven into the adjacent piece at an angle. Popular modern technique thanks to Kreg Jig.

‚úì Pros

  • Very fast
  • Easy for beginners
  • No clamps needed
  • Strong enough for most

‚úó Cons

  • Visible holes
  • Not traditional
  • Requires special jig
  • Less refined look

Best For:

Face frames, quick projects, cabinet construction, furniture with hidden areas

Tools Needed:

Pocket hole jig (Kreg Jig), drill, special screws, glue

Rabbet Joint

BEGINNER
Strength:

An L-shaped groove cut along the edge or end of a board. The second piece fits into this recess, creating more glue surface and a stronger connection than a butt joint.

‚úì Pros

  • Stronger than butt
  • More glue surface
  • Easy to make
  • Hides one end grain

‚úó Cons

  • Still needs fasteners
  • Moderate strength only
  • Groove may be visible
  • Not for heavy loads

Best For:

Cabinet backs, drawer bottoms, simple boxes, picture frame backs

Tools Needed:

Router, dado blade, or multiple passes with table saw

Half-Lap Joint

INTERMEDIATE
Strength:

Half the thickness is removed from each piece so they overlap and sit flush. Creates a strong joint with good glue surface while maintaining the full thickness.

‚úì Pros

  • Strong and rigid
  • Flush surface
  • Large glue area
  • Versatile angles

‚úó Cons

  • Waste material
  • Must be precise depth
  • Takes more time
  • Visible joint line

Best For:

Picture frames, face frames, furniture stretchers, cross bracing

Tools Needed:

Table saw or router, chisel for cleanup, dado blade helpful

Biscuit Joint

BEGINNER
Strength:

Football-shaped compressed wood wafers (biscuits) fit into matching crescent-shaped slots. The biscuits swell when glue is applied, creating a tight fit.

‚úì Pros

  • Fast and easy
  • Great for alignment
  • Hidden joint
  • Adds strength

‚úó Cons

  • Needs biscuit joiner
  • Not as strong as M&T
  • Biscuits cost money
  • Limited applications

Best For:

Edge-to-edge glue-ups, miter reinforcement, cabinet face frames, quick assembly

Tools Needed:

Biscuit joiner (plate joiner), biscuits, glue

Tongue & Groove

INTERMEDIATE
Strength:

A raised rib (tongue) on one piece fits into a matching groove on another. Allows for wood movement while keeping pieces aligned. Classic flooring joint.

‚úì Pros

  • Perfect alignment
  • Allows wood movement
  • Large glue surface
  • Hidden fasteners

‚úó Cons

  • Requires router/shaper
  • Time-consuming setup
  • Loses some width
  • Matching bits needed

Best For:

Flooring, paneling, table tops, wide glue-ups, cabinet backs

Tools Needed:

Router with tongue & groove bits, or shaper, or special table saw setup

üí° Joint Selection Guide

For Beginners: Start with butt joints reinforced with pocket screws, then progress to miter joints and dados.
For Speed: Pocket holes and biscuits are the fastest strong joints. Great for production work.
For Strength: Mortise & tenon and dovetails are the strongest. Half-laps and box joints are excellent compromises.
For Appearance: Dovetails and box joints showcase craftsmanship. Dowels and biscuits are completely hidden.
For Drawers: Dovetails (traditional), box joints (strong and beautiful), or dado/rabbet combinations (practical).

üîß General Joint-Making Tips

  • Test Fit First: Always do a dry assembly before glue
  • Sharp Tools: Sharp chisels and saw blades = clean joints
  • Measure Twice: Accuracy is everything in joinery
  • Glue Surface: More glue surface = stronger joint
  • Wood Movement: Consider seasonal expansion/contraction
  • Practice: Make test joints in scrap wood first
  • Proper Clamping: Use enough clamps, don't over-tighten
  • Glue Squeeze-Out: Remove while still wet or after fully dry
