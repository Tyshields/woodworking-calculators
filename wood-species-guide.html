üå≤ Wood Species Guide

Properties, characteristics, and best uses for common woodworking lumber

HARDWOOD

Oak (Red & White)

Hardness: 1290 (Red) / 1360 (White)
Color: Light to medium brown
Grain: Prominent, open
Workability: Good (pre-drill for nails)
Durability: Excellent (White is rot-resistant)

Best For:

  • Furniture (durable, traditional)
  • Flooring
  • Cabinets
  • Outdoor projects (White Oak)
  • Trim and molding
HARDWOOD

Maple (Hard & Soft)

Hardness: 1450 (Hard) / 950 (Soft)
Color: Creamy white to light tan
Grain: Fine, even, sometimes figured
Workability: Good (can burn easily)
Finish: Excellent (takes stain well)

Best For:

  • Cutting boards and butcher blocks
  • Flooring (extremely durable)
  • Cabinets and furniture
  • Musical instruments
  • Turning projects
SOFTWOOD

Pine (White, Yellow, Southern)

Hardness: 380-690 (varies by type)
Color: Pale yellow to light brown
Grain: Straight with visible knots
Workability: Excellent (easy to cut)
Cost: Budget-friendly

Best For:

  • Beginner projects
  • Framing and construction
  • Painted furniture
  • Shelving
  • Rustic/farmhouse style
HARDWOOD

Cherry (Black Cherry)

Hardness: 995
Color: Light pink to rich reddish-brown
Grain: Fine, straight, satiny
Workability: Excellent (machines beautifully)
Aging: Darkens beautifully over time

Best For:

  • Fine furniture
  • Cabinets (premium)
  • Musical instruments
  • Turning projects
  • Heirloom pieces
HARDWOOD

Walnut (Black Walnut)

Hardness: 1010
Color: Dark chocolate brown
Grain: Straight, sometimes wavy
Workability: Excellent (premium wood)
Cost: High (premium hardwood)

Best For:

  • High-end furniture
  • Gunstocks
  • Cutting boards
  • Accent pieces
  • Veneer work
HARDWOOD

Poplar (Yellow Poplar)

Hardness: 540
Color: Creamy white to greenish
Grain: Straight, uniform
Workability: Excellent (easy to work)
Cost: Affordable hardwood

Best For:

  • Painted projects
  • Cabinet interiors
  • Practice projects
  • Drawer boxes
  • Trim (painted)
SOFTWOOD

Cedar (Western Red Cedar)

Hardness: 350
Color: Reddish-brown
Grain: Straight with knots
Durability: Excellent (rot & insect resistant)
Aroma: Pleasant, distinctive

Best For:

  • Outdoor furniture
  • Decking
  • Fencing
  • Closet lining
  • Siding
HARDWOOD

Ash (White Ash)

Hardness: 1320
Color: Light brown to nearly white
Grain: Straight, open, pronounced
Strength: Excellent (shock resistant)
Workability: Good (machines well)

Best For:

  • Tool handles
  • Baseball bats & sports equipment
  • Flooring
  • Furniture frames
  • Bent wood projects

üí° Wood Selection Tips

For Beginners: Start with Pine or Poplar - they're affordable, easy to work with, and forgiving of mistakes.
For Outdoor Projects: Choose Cedar, White Oak, or Teak - they resist rot and insects naturally.
For Painted Projects: Poplar is ideal - smooth surface, takes paint beautifully, and costs less than other hardwoods.
For Cutting Boards: Use Maple, Walnut, or Cherry - they're food-safe, hard, and non-porous.
For Fine Furniture: Cherry, Walnut, or Maple offer beautiful grain and durability for heirloom pieces.

üìè Understanding Hardness Ratings

Janka Hardness Scale measures the force needed to embed a steel ball into wood:

  • Under 700: Soft (dents easily, good for carving)
  • 700-1200: Medium (good all-purpose woods)
  • 1200-1500: Hard (excellent for flooring, furniture)
  • Over 1500: Very Hard (premium, highly durable)

üå≥ Hardwood vs Softwood

Common Misconception: The terms don't refer to actual hardness!

  • Hardwoods: Come from deciduous trees (lose leaves in winter) - Oak, Maple, Cherry, Walnut
  • Softwoods: Come from coniferous trees (evergreens) - Pine, Cedar, Spruce, Fir
  • Fun Fact: Poplar is technically a hardwood but softer than many softwoods!
